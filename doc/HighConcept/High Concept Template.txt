In general a high concept document should be no more than two pages long and should take no more than 10 minutes to read. It shouldn¡¯t have a title page; the title and your name appear at the top of the first page, and the text begins immediately. Its most important material must appear on the first page.
In the sections that follow, we describe the key elements of a high concept document.
* High Concept.  After the title and your name, the document should begin with no more than two lines that state the idea of the game. In a commercial environment, it is imperative that the idea be instantly comprehensible because everyone¡¯s most precious commodity is time. If the producer doesn¡¯t get the idea in a sentence or two, he¡¯s going to worry that the publisher¡¯s sales staff, the wholesale buyers, and, most important, the retail customers won¡¯t get it either. If you¡¯re going to propose something really strange for a retail game, you need to be very good at explaining it!
* Genre. Indicate the genre of the game, or if it is a mix of genres, indicate that.
* Main Features.  Next you should include a bulleted list of the key features of the game. Each item should consist of two or three sentences, no more. Remember that since you are not including concept drawings, your reader doesn¡¯t have a mental picture of the game, so this section needs to build one for him. It¡¯s much more important at this point to convey the game¡¯s look and feel than to give the details about how it works. You¡¯re not selling the game¡¯s internal economy or its AI; you¡¯re selling the player¡¯s experience. Avoid letting the feature list run on to the next page, if you can. More than about 10 bullet points in a row starts to look like a mass of text to read and discourages the eye. Next you need to summarize the key commercial considerations about the game: what machine it¡¯s for, who would buy it and why, and what you¡¯re hoping that the game will achieve. Consider including any or all the following items:
* Player Challenge/Motivation. This is a short statement that indicates what the player is trying to accomplish in the game¡ªhis role and goal. This helps indicate what sort of person the player is. He can be driven by a desire to compete, to solve puzzles, to explore, or whatever.
* Design goals. In this section, list your aims for the game as an experience. Don¡¯t just say ¡°fun¡±¡ªthat¡¯s too broad. Be more specific. Are you trying to provide pulse-pounding excitement? * Target customer. What kind of person will buy this game? If age or sex is relevant, indicate that; more important, tell what other kinds of games they like to play.
* Unique selling points. What¡¯s new in this game? How will it stand out from what has gone before?

* Target hardware. Tell what machine the game is intended for. Also indicate whether the game requires or can make optional use of any special hardware or accessories.
* License. If you intend for the game to exploit a licensed property, say so here. Also include any facts and figures about the property¡¯s popularity, recognition value, and appeal to particular markets¡ªbut no more than a sentence or two.
Further Details (Optional)
In the last section, you can include additional material that you think the reader will enjoy learning about the game.
